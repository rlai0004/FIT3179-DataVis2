2023 University World Rankings and Student Insights

Welcome to our journey through the academic world of 2023, where we delve into university rankings and student statistics.

The Times Higher Education World University Rankings 2023 include 1,799 universities across 104 countries and regions, making them the largest and most diverse university rankings to date.

These rankings are based on 13 carefully calibrated performance indicators that measure an institutionâ€™s performance across four areas: teaching, research, knowledge transfer, and international outlook.

Let's explore the data and see what we can find out about the world of academia in 2023.

Exploring Global Student Numbers

Before delving into the world of university rankings, let's take a closer look at the global student population and how it is distributed across countries.

As evident in the accompanying bubble chart, there's a noticeable correlation between the number of students and universities in a country, as one would logically expect.

Most countries, particularly smaller countries with populations less than 200 million, tend to have a proportional number of students and universities, with up to 1 million students and 60 universities.

However, there are some countries that stand out from the rest:

  • The United States of America boasts the highest number of universities (179) and students (4 million) globally, despite its smaller population compared to countries like China and India.
  • The United Kingdom and Japan have a substantial number of universities (over 150) but not as many students in comparison.
  • Russia and India also have around 100 universities each but only about 1 million students enrolled.
  • Egypt, on the other hand, has a relatively high number of students (2.6 million) with a comparatively lower number of universities (40).

    • The choropleth map below provides another useful view of student distribution around the world, normalising the number of students in each country by its population.

    From the choropleth map, we can see that the countries with the highest student-to-population ratios are Greece, Iceland and Australia, each with over 300 students per 10,000 in population.

    Generally, student enrollment per capita seems highest in western nations located in western Europe, Oceania and North America . Asian and African nations generally have lower student-to-population ratios, with some exceptions such as Japan, Turkey and Egypt.

    University Ranking Distribution by Region

    Now, let's examine the global distribution of university rankings. As evident from the two charts to the right, Oceania stands out with a median rank of 298, and nearly all of its universities boasting rankings better than the global median. Remarkably, 80% of universities in Oceania are positioned within the top 500 globally.

    Apart from Oceania, both the Americas and Europe exhibit similar ranking distributions, with a significant presence of universities at the top echelons of the university ranking leaderboard. Approximately 35% and 37% of universities in the Americas and Europe, respectively, secure places within the top 500 globally.

    In contrast, Asia and Africa share comparable ranking distributions, with a smaller percentage of universities ranked in the top 500 globally (approximately 10% and 15%, respectively). Instead, a more substantial proportion of universities in these regions falls within the range of ranks 1,001 to 1,500.

    University Size Distribution and its Impact on Rankings

    Now, let's explore the distribution of university sizes around the world and understand how it influences university rankings. Focusing on the top 500 universities globally, we find that the Fmajority of these institutions have a student population of less than 40,000, with a median size of just over 22,000 students.

    Surprisingly, there doesn't appear to be a strong relationship between university size and their global rankings. However, when adjusting the slider on the histogram and heatmap to view more universities, it becomes evident that very large universities tend not to perform well in global rankings.

    The distribution hints that maintaining a relatively moderate size, with a range of 10,000 to 40,000 students, is often the optimal choice for most universities. This is further emphasized by the fact that 36 of the top 50 universities fall within this size range.

    International Students and Their Impact on University Rankings

    Finally, let's delve into the distribution of international students around the world and explore its connection to university rankings. Examining the scatterplots, we observe a notable correlation between the percentage of international students and university rank in most regions. This intriguing relationship opens the door to several interpretations.

    It may suggest that universities with a strong international outreach tend to perform better in university rankings, emphasizing the importance of global diversity. On the other hand, it might also indicate that students are naturally drawn to universities that have already established strong positions in rankings.

    This relationship prompts a compelling question: Can universities enhance their rankings by investing more in international outreach? Further analysis could shed light on this matter.

    Of particular interest is the distinct profile of African universities. They stand out by maintaining a significantly lower proportion of international students compared to universities in other regions. All African universities in our dataset have less than 20% international students, setting them apart in this aspect. This is likely due to external factors and reasons that students around the world are less interested in studying in Africa - another area of interest for further exploration.
